# Marketing Ideas and Growth Experiments

## Idea Generation Frameworks

### The Channel Brainstorm Matrix
Systematically generate ideas by crossing your marketing assets against distribution channels.

**Assets (rows):**
- Your product itself
- Customer data and insights
- Content and expertise
- Brand and reputation
- Existing audience
- Partnerships and relationships
- Budget

**Channels (columns):**
- Organic search (SEO)
- Paid search (SEM)
- Social media (organic)
- Social media (paid)
- Email
- Content marketing
- Community
- Partnerships
- Events
- PR and media
- Referral and word-of-mouth
- Product-led growth
- Direct outreach

For each cell, ask: "How could we use [asset] through [channel] to acquire or retain customers?" Not every cell will yield a viable idea, but the exercise ensures you consider the full solution space.

### The 10x vs 10% Framework
Separate your ideas into two categories:

**10% improvements (Optimization):**
- A/B test your landing page headline
- Improve email subject lines
- Optimize ad creative
- Refine your targeting parameters
- Speed up your signup flow

**10x ideas (Step changes):**
- Build a free tool that attracts your target audience
- Create an industry benchmark report with original data
- Launch a podcast interviewing your customers' customers
- Build a community that becomes the default gathering place for your niche
- Partner with a complementary product for co-marketing

Balance your portfolio: 70% of effort on proven channels (optimization), 20% on adjacent experiments, 10% on wild ideas.

### The Customer Journey Audit
Walk through every step of your customer's journey and identify friction points and opportunities:

1. **Unaware** — They do not know they have a problem. How do you create awareness?
2. **Problem-aware** — They know the problem but not the solution. How do you educate?
3. **Solution-aware** — They know solutions exist but not yours. How do you get discovered?
4. **Product-aware** — They know you exist but have not tried you. How do you convert?
5. **Active user** — They are using your product. How do you deepen engagement?
6. **Champion** — They love your product. How do you turn them into advocates?

For each stage, brainstorm 5-10 tactics. The biggest opportunities are usually at the stages you are ignoring.

### Competitor Reverse Engineering
Study what your competitors are doing, then identify what they are NOT doing.

- Run their domain through Ahrefs/Semrush: What keywords drive their traffic? What content formats do they use?
- Subscribe to their email lists: What is their nurture sequence? What offers do they make?
- Follow their social accounts: What content gets engagement? What falls flat?
- Sign up for their product: What does their onboarding look like? Where do they try to upsell?
- Read their reviews: What do customers love? What do they complain about?

The ideas are in the gaps between what they do and what they miss.

## Growth Experiment Design

### The Experiment Brief Template
Every experiment should be documented before execution:

**Experiment Name:** A short, memorable name (e.g., "LinkedIn Carousel Series" or "Exit Intent Popup Test").

**Hypothesis:** "We believe that [action] will result in [outcome] because [rationale]."
- Bad: "We think LinkedIn will work."
- Good: "We believe that publishing weekly LinkedIn carousels summarizing customer case studies will generate 50+ qualified profile visits per week because our ICP (marketing directors) actively engages with visual content on LinkedIn."

**Metric:** The single number that determines success or failure. Be specific: "Increase free trial signups from LinkedIn from 12/month to 30/month."

**Minimum Viable Test:** The smallest version of this experiment that can validate or invalidate the hypothesis. Do not build a full campaign — build the minimum to learn.

**Timeline:** How long will you run the experiment? Most marketing experiments need 2-4 weeks to generate statistically meaningful data.

**Resources:** What do you need? Time, budget, tools, creative assets, team members.

**Kill criteria:** At what point do you stop the experiment early? Define this upfront to avoid sunk-cost thinking. "If we see zero signups after 2 weeks, we stop."

### Minimum Viable Tests for Common Channels

**Content marketing:**
- Write 3 articles targeting high-intent keywords. Measure organic traffic after 60 days.
- Minimum viable: Publish on your blog with basic on-page SEO.

**Paid social:**
- Run $500 in ads across 3 audiences and 3 ad creatives. Measure cost per click and cost per signup.
- Minimum viable: Use existing content as ad creative. Do not produce new video.

**Email outreach:**
- Send 100 personalized cold emails to a tightly defined ICP. Measure reply rate and meeting rate.
- Minimum viable: Send manually from your personal email. Do not buy tools yet.

**Community:**
- Spend 30 minutes/day for 2 weeks contributing to 3 communities where your ICP hangs out. Track referral traffic and signups.
- Minimum viable: Use your personal account. Share insights, not product links.

**Partnerships:**
- Reach out to 10 complementary products and propose a co-marketing initiative. Track response rate and resulting traffic.
- Minimum viable: A mutual email mention or social media cross-post.

## Prioritization with ICE Scoring

### The ICE Framework
Score every experiment on three dimensions from 1-10:

**Impact (1-10):** If this works, how much will it move the needle?
- 1-3: Minor improvement to a secondary metric
- 4-6: Meaningful improvement to a primary metric
- 7-10: Step change in growth trajectory

**Confidence (1-10):** How confident are you that this will work?
- 1-3: Pure speculation, no data or precedent
- 4-6: Some supporting evidence (competitor does it, adjacent data suggests it)
- 7-10: Strong evidence (you have tested something similar, or industry benchmarks support it)

**Ease (1-10):** How easy is this to execute?
- 1-3: Requires significant engineering, budget, or time (months)
- 4-6: Moderate effort, can be done in 1-2 weeks with existing resources
- 7-10: Can be done in a day or two with no special resources

**ICE Score = (Impact + Confidence + Ease) / 3**

Sort experiments by ICE score. Run the top 2-3 experiments per sprint (2-week cycle). Review results at the end of each sprint.

### Common Scoring Pitfalls
- **Confidence inflation:** Everyone overestimates confidence. Force yourself to cite evidence.
- **Impact without specificity:** "This could be huge" is not impact analysis. Estimate the actual metric impact.
- **Ease underestimation:** Include the full cost — not just execution but also design, review, QA, and iteration.
- **Ignoring compounding:** Some experiments have low immediate impact but compound over time (SEO, content). Weight these separately.

## Rapid Experimentation Mindset

### The Experimentation Cadence
- **Weekly:** Review active experiment metrics. Kill failures fast.
- **Bi-weekly:** Complete one experiment cycle. Document learnings. Launch next batch.
- **Monthly:** Review the experiment backlog. Re-score ideas based on new data. Present learnings to the team.
- **Quarterly:** Analyze which channels and tactics have the highest ROI. Shift budget accordingly. Archive dead ideas. Generate a new batch.

### Learning Over Winning
Not every experiment needs to "work." The goal is learning velocity — how fast you accumulate knowledge about what works for your specific product, market, and audience.

A failed experiment that teaches you "our ICP does not respond to cold email" is more valuable than a successful experiment you cannot explain. Document every experiment's outcome, regardless of result.

### The 80/20 Rule for Experiments
After 3-6 months of experimentation, you will find that 1-2 channels drive 80% of your growth. Once you identify these, shift from broad experimentation to deep optimization. But never stop running small experiments on new channels — your winning channels will eventually saturate.

## Channel-Specific Ideas

### Content Marketing Ideas
- **Original research reports:** Survey your users or scrape public data to create an annual industry report. These earn backlinks, press coverage, and authority.
- **Interactive tools and calculators:** Build a simple calculator that helps your ICP quantify a problem your product solves. (See: free-tool-strategy skill.)
- **Comparison and alternative content:** Create "X vs Y" and "Best alternatives to X" content targeting high-intent keywords.
- **Template libraries:** Offer free templates (spreadsheets, documents, design files) that showcase your product's use cases.
- **Customer story spotlights:** Go beyond case studies. Tell the human story of how a customer solved a real problem.

### Community-Led Ideas
- **Host a niche Slack or Discord community:** Become the default gathering place for your industry vertical.
- **Run a weekly AMA or office hours:** Invite industry experts and rotate topics.
- **Create a curated newsletter:** Aggregate the best content, tools, and insights for your niche. Build an audience before selling.
- **Launch a challenge or cohort program:** "30-day marketing challenge" or "Growth Sprint Cohort" — structured programs that drive engagement and word-of-mouth.

### Product-Led Ideas
- **Viral loops in the product:** Add share triggers at moments of delight. "Share your results" after completing an analysis. "Invite your team" during onboarding.
- **Powered-by badges:** If your product generates output (reports, websites, emails), include a subtle attribution link.
- **Public profiles or portfolios:** Let users create public-facing content with your tool that links back to you.
- **Integrations as distribution:** Build integrations with tools your ICP already uses. Each integration is a co-marketing opportunity.

### Outbound Ideas
- **Personalized video outreach:** Record 30-second Loom videos for high-value prospects. Response rates are 3-5x higher than text-only emails.
- **Trigger-based outreach:** Monitor signals (new funding, new hires, tech stack changes) and reach out with relevant messaging.
- **LinkedIn thought leadership:** Post daily insights related to the problem you solve. Build authority before pitching.
- **Podcast guesting:** Pitch yourself as a guest on podcasts your ICP listens to. Prepare a specific, actionable topic — not a product pitch.

### Unconventional Ideas
- **Sponsor open-source projects** your ICP uses. High goodwill, low cost, targeted audience.
- **Create a swipe file or resource library** — curate the best examples of marketing emails, landing pages, or ad creatives in your industry.
- **Run a micro-conference or virtual summit** — 4-6 speakers, 1 day, free attendance. Capture email addresses and build authority.
- **Publish your internal playbooks** — Share how you do marketing, hiring, product development. Radical transparency builds trust and attracts like-minded customers.
- **Build in public** — Share your revenue numbers, growth challenges, and product decisions publicly. This works exceptionally well for bootstrapped SaaS.
- **Create an "impossible" guarantee** — Offer a guarantee that feels risky but is backed by your product's actual performance. "Hit X goal in 90 days or we refund you."
- **Reverse demo days** — Instead of demoing your product, invite prospects to demo their workflow. You learn their pain points; they feel heard.

## Evaluating Results

### Metrics That Matter by Stage

**Awareness stage experiments:**
- Impressions, reach, brand mention volume
- New visitors, traffic from new channels
- Social engagement rate (not vanity metrics — engagement that leads to clicks)

**Consideration stage experiments:**
- Marketing qualified leads (MQLs)
- Content engagement (time on page, scroll depth, return visits)
- Email list growth rate and engagement

**Conversion stage experiments:**
- Signup rate, trial starts, demo requests
- Cost per acquisition (CPA) by channel
- Time to convert from first touch to signup

**Retention and expansion experiments:**
- Activation rate (percentage reaching the aha moment)
- Net revenue retention
- Referral rate and viral coefficient

### When to Scale a Winning Experiment
An experiment is ready to scale when:
1. The result is statistically significant (not just one good week).
2. You understand WHY it works, not just THAT it works.
3. The unit economics are positive (CAC < LTV by at least 3x).
4. You have a plan to maintain quality at higher volume.
5. Scaling will not cannibalize your other channels.

### When to Kill a Losing Experiment
Kill an experiment when:
1. You have reached your pre-defined kill criteria.
2. The data shows no signal after a reasonable test period (not just noise — there is a difference between "no signal" and "inconclusive").
3. The cost of continuing exceeds the potential learning value.
4. Your hypothesis has been clearly invalidated.

Do not kill experiments because of impatience. SEO experiments need 60-90 days. Community experiments need 30-60 days. Paid experiments need 2-4 weeks with sufficient budget.
