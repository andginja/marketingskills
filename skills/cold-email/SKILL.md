# B2B Cold Email

## Purpose

Write cold emails that earn replies from busy people who do not know you. The bar is high: your email competes with 50-100 other emails in the recipient's inbox. Every sentence must justify its existence, and the email must feel like it was written by a peer, not a salesperson.

## Core Philosophy

Cold email is not about selling. It is about starting a conversation. The only goal of a cold email is to get a reply — not to close a deal, not to explain your product, not to dump your pitch. A reply. That's it.

## Step 1: Gather Context Before Writing

### About the Recipient
- **Who are they?** (Job title, company, industry, company size)
- **What do they care about?** (KPIs they own, problems they face, goals they're measured on)
- **What have they done recently?** (Funding round, new hire, product launch, conference talk, LinkedIn post, job change)
- **Why would they be open to this conversation right now?** (Trigger event, timing signal, seasonal relevance)

### About the Offer
- **What problem do you solve?** (In one sentence, from the buyer's perspective)
- **Who have you solved it for?** (Similar companies, recognizable names, same industry)
- **What's the proof?** (Specific result: percentage, dollar amount, time saved)
- **What's the ask?** (15-minute call, async demo, share a resource, intro to the right person)

## Step 2: Writing Principles

### Principle 1: Write Like a Peer, Not a Vendor
- No "Dear [First Name]," — use "Hi [First Name]," or just start talking.
- No corporate language. Write like you'd message a colleague.
- No "I hope this email finds you well." It finds them annoyed.
- No "I'd love to" or "I'd be thrilled to." Just state what you want.

### Principle 2: Every Sentence Must Earn the Next Sentence
- The subject line's job is to get the email opened.
- The first sentence's job is to get the second sentence read.
- The last sentence's job is to get a reply.
- If any sentence does not serve one of these goals, cut it.

### Principle 3: Personalization Must Connect to the Problem
- Bad personalization: "I saw you went to Stanford. Go Cardinals!"
- Good personalization: "I saw you just hired 3 SDRs. When teams scale outbound that fast, pipeline tracking usually breaks first."
- Personalization should demonstrate that you understand their world, not that you stalked their LinkedIn.

### Principle 4: Short Is a Feature
- Target 50-100 words for the body. Never exceed 150.
- 3-5 sentences total.
- If your email takes more than 15 seconds to read on a phone screen, it is too long.

### Principle 5: One Ask, Clearly Stated
- Do not give the reader choices. Do not ask them to "check out our website or book a call or reply with questions."
- One clear, low-friction ask. Make it easy to say yes.

## Step 3: Structure Frameworks

### Framework 1: Observation - Problem - Proof - Ask

This is the most versatile framework. It works for most B2B cold emails.

```
[Observation about their specific situation]
[Connect it to a problem they likely face]
[Brief proof you can solve it — one sentence, one stat]
[Clear, specific ask]
```

**Example:**
```
Hi Sarah,

Noticed Acme just launched a self-serve tier — congrats. In my
experience, the support volume spike hits about 6 weeks after
launch, and most teams aren't ready for it.

We helped Brex cut support tickets by 40% during their self-serve
rollout using automated onboarding flows.

Worth a 15-minute call to see if that's relevant for you?
```

### Framework 2: Question - Value - Ask

Lead with a question that surfaces a pain point they recognize.

```
[Question about a problem they likely face]
[How you've solved it for someone similar]
[Ask]
```

**Example:**
```
Hi Marcus,

How's your team handling pipeline attribution now that you're
running campaigns across 5+ channels?

We built an attribution model for Lattice that showed them 30%
of their pipeline was coming from a channel they almost cut.

If that's a challenge for you too, happy to share what we learned.
15 minutes?
```

### Framework 3: Trigger - Insight - Ask

Use a specific trigger event as the entry point.

```
[Reference to a trigger event — funding, hire, launch, etc.]
[Insight about what typically happens next]
[Ask]
```

**Example:**
```
Hi Dana,

Saw the Series B announcement — congrats on the raise. In our
experience, the first thing that breaks at your stage is the
finance stack. What worked for a 20-person team doesn't survive
scaling to 80.

We helped Notion rebuild their finance ops during a similar
growth phase. Happy to share the playbook over a quick call.
```

### Framework 4: Referral / Warm Intro Angle

Leverage a mutual connection or shared context.

```
[Mention how you're connected]
[Relevant problem or insight]
[Ask]
```

**Example:**
```
Hi James,

[Mutual connection] mentioned you're rebuilding your data
pipeline. We helped their team migrate from Segment to a
custom CDP in 6 weeks — half the timeline they expected.

Would it be useful to compare notes? Happy to do 15 minutes
this week.
```

## Step 4: Subject Lines

### Rules
- 1-5 words. Shorter is better. The best subject lines look like internal emails.
- Lowercase. No title case. No exclamation marks.
- No "Quick question" — it's been ruined by overuse.
- No emojis.
- Never mislead. The subject must relate to the email content.

### Patterns That Work
| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| [Their company] + [topic] | "Acme + attribution" |
| Question (short) | "pipeline tracking?" |
| Mutual connection | "from [name]'s recommendation" |
| Trigger reference | "re: the Series B" |
| Direct and plain | "onboarding support volume" |
| One word | "attribution" |

### Subject Lines to Avoid
- "Exciting opportunity for [Company]" — sounds like spam.
- "Can I get 15 minutes?" — leads with your need, not theirs.
- "Increase your revenue by 300%" — unbelievable and spammy.
- "[First Name], I have an idea for you" — manipulative.
- RE: or FWD: when there was no prior thread — dishonest.

## Step 5: Follow-Up Sequences

### Sequence Structure (3-5 Emails)
Do not send the same email again. Each follow-up should introduce a new angle or piece of value.

**Email 1 (Day 0):** Primary outreach. Use one of the frameworks above.

**Email 2 (Day 3-4):** Add new value. Share a relevant insight, resource, or proof point. Do not say "just following up" or "bumping this to the top of your inbox."

```
Hi Sarah,

Thought this might be relevant — we published a breakdown of
how self-serve onboarding affects support load in the first 90
days: [link]

The TL;DR: teams that automate the first 3 onboarding steps
see 35-50% fewer tickets.

Still happy to chat if this is on your radar.
```

**Email 3 (Day 7-8):** Different angle. Approach the problem from a new direction, or reference a different pain point.

```
Hi Sarah,

One more thought — beyond support volume, the other thing we
see post-launch is a spike in "how do I do X" questions that
your docs don't cover yet.

Our tool auto-generates contextual help from your existing
knowledge base. Takes about a day to set up.

Worth exploring?
```

**Email 4 (Day 14):** Social proof or case study. Share a specific result from a similar company.

```
Hi Sarah,

Quick data point: Ramp launched self-serve in Q3 and used our
platform to handle the onboarding flow. They kept support
headcount flat while tripling their user base.

Happy to share the details if useful. If not, no worries — I'll
stop filling your inbox.
```

**Email 5 (Day 21-28):** Breakup email. Give them an easy out. Paradoxically, breakup emails often get the highest reply rate.

```
Hi Sarah,

I'll keep this short — I've reached out a few times about
managing support load after your self-serve launch.

If the timing isn't right, totally understand. If it becomes
relevant later, my calendar's always open: [link]

Either way, good luck with the rollout.
```

### Angle Rotation Across Emails
Each follow-up should take a different angle:
- **Email 1:** Problem + proof
- **Email 2:** Educational resource or insight
- **Email 3:** Different pain point or use case
- **Email 4:** Social proof / case study
- **Email 5:** Graceful close

Never repeat the same angle. If you run out of angles, you don't understand the prospect well enough.

## Step 6: Quality Checklist

Before sending, verify every email passes these checks:

### Content
- [ ] Is the personalization connected to a real problem, not just flattery?
- [ ] Is there a clear reason this email is relevant to them right now?
- [ ] Does the proof point include a specific number, company, or outcome?
- [ ] Is the ask clear, specific, and low-friction?
- [ ] Would this email make sense if you removed the first line? (If yes, your personalization is decorative — fix it.)

### Format
- [ ] Under 100 words? (150 absolute max)
- [ ] 3-5 sentences?
- [ ] No paragraphs longer than 2 sentences?
- [ ] No bullet points or formatting? (Plain text performs better in cold email.)
- [ ] No images, logos, or HTML signatures?

### Tone
- [ ] Does it sound like a peer, not a salesperson?
- [ ] No "I hope this finds you well" or similar filler?
- [ ] No "I'd love to" or "I'd be thrilled"?
- [ ] No exclamation marks?
- [ ] Would you send this to a friend who works at another company?

### Technical
- [ ] Subject line under 5 words?
- [ ] No spam trigger words? (free, guarantee, act now, limited time)
- [ ] Personalization fields correctly populated? (No "[First Name]" errors)
- [ ] Unsubscribe or opt-out option present? (Required by law in most jurisdictions)

## Step 7: What to Avoid

### Never Do These
1. **The feature dump.** "Our platform offers real-time analytics, custom dashboards, API integrations, team collaboration tools, and automated reporting." Nobody reads this.
2. **The ego open.** "We're a fast-growing startup backed by [VC firm] that's disrupting the [industry] space." They don't care about you yet.
3. **The guilt trip follow-up.** "I've sent you 3 emails and haven't heard back..." You're not owed a reply.
4. **The fake personalization.** "I love what you're doing at [Company]!" Without specifics, this reads as a mail merge.
5. **The novel.** If your email requires scrolling on a phone, it's too long. Cut it in half, then cut it again.
6. **The multi-ask.** "Would you like to book a demo, read our case study, or connect me with your head of engineering?" Pick one.
7. **The premature pitch.** Explaining your product in detail before establishing relevance. Nobody reads unsolicited product descriptions.
8. **Lying or misleading.** Fake "Re:" subject lines, false mutual connections, fabricated stats. This destroys trust instantly and permanently.

## Output Format

When writing cold emails, deliver:

1. **Subject line** — 2-3 options ranked by preference.
2. **Email body** — the primary version, ready to send.
3. **Alternative opening** — a second option for the first 1-2 sentences.
4. **Follow-up sequence** — at minimum, emails 2 and 3 with suggested send timing.
5. **Personalization notes** — what to research and customize for each prospect.
6. **Assumptions** — list what you assumed about the recipient and offer, so the user can correct.
