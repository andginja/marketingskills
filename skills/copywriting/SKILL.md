# Conversion Copywriting

## Purpose

Write high-converting copy for websites, landing pages, and product pages. Every word must earn its place. The goal is clarity, credibility, and action — not cleverness.

## Step 1: Gather Context Before Writing

Never write copy without understanding these five inputs. Ask the user if any are missing.

### 1. Page Purpose
- What is the single action this page should drive? (sign up, book a demo, buy, download)
- Where does this page sit in the funnel? (awareness, consideration, decision)

### 2. Audience
- Who is the reader? (job title, seniority, company size, industry)
- What is their current situation? (pain points, frustrations, failed alternatives)
- What does success look like for them?
- What objections will they have?

### 3. Product / Offer
- What does the product do in one sentence?
- What are the top 3 outcomes it delivers?
- What proof exists? (stats, logos, testimonials, case studies)
- What makes it different from alternatives?

### 4. Traffic Source
- Where is the reader coming from? (ad, organic search, referral, email)
- What message or promise brought them here?
- What level of awareness do they have? (unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, product-aware)

### 5. Brand Voice
- Formal or conversational?
- Technical or accessible?
- Any words or phrases to use or avoid?

## Step 2: Core Copywriting Principles

### Clarity Over Cleverness
- If a reader has to re-read a sentence to understand it, rewrite it.
- Avoid puns, wordplay, and abstract metaphors in headlines.
- The reader should understand what you do, who it's for, and why it matters within 5 seconds of landing on the page.

### Benefits Over Features
- A feature is what the product does. A benefit is what the customer gets.
- Always lead with the benefit. Mention the feature as supporting evidence.
- Bad: "AI-powered analytics engine"
- Good: "Know exactly which campaigns drive revenue — with AI that connects the dots for you"

### Specificity Beats Generality
- Vague claims create doubt. Specific claims create trust.
- Bad: "Save time on reporting"
- Good: "Cut weekly reporting from 4 hours to 15 minutes"
- Use real numbers, timeframes, and outcomes whenever possible.

### Use the Customer's Language
- Mirror the exact words your audience uses to describe their problems.
- Pull language from support tickets, sales calls, reviews, and survey responses.
- Avoid internal jargon, product codenames, and marketing buzzwords.

### One Idea Per Section
- Each section of the page should make one clear point.
- Do not stack multiple arguments into a single paragraph.
- Let whitespace and structure do the heavy lifting.

## Step 3: Writing Style Rules

### Sentence Structure
- Lead with the most important information.
- Keep sentences under 20 words when possible.
- Vary sentence length to create rhythm: short sentence. Then a longer one that expands the idea. Then short again.
- Use fragments deliberately for emphasis. Like this.

### Word Choice
- Prefer short, common words over long, formal ones.
- "Use" not "utilize." "Start" not "commence." "Help" not "facilitate."
- Cut adverbs. If the verb needs an adverb, pick a stronger verb.
- Remove hedge words: "just," "really," "very," "actually," "basically."

### Formatting for Scanners
- 80% of readers scan. Structure copy for scanning first, reading second.
- Use bold text to highlight key phrases a scanner should catch.
- Break text into short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max).
- Use bullet points for lists of 3+ items.
- Subheadings should be meaningful, not decorative. A reader who only reads subheadings should understand the full argument.

### Pronouns
- Use "you" and "your" — talk directly to the reader.
- Use "we" sparingly and only when establishing credibility.
- Never use "users" or "customers" when addressing the audience directly.

## Step 4: Page Structure Framework

### Above the Fold (Hero Section)
This is the most important real estate on the page. It must answer three questions instantly:
1. What is this? (headline)
2. Why should I care? (subheadline)
3. What do I do next? (CTA)

**Headline patterns that work:**
- Outcome-driven: "[Achieve outcome] without [pain point]"
- Clarity-first: "[Product category] that [key differentiator]"
- Audience-specific: "[Role/persona]: [promise that resonates]"
- Proof-led: "[Number] [companies/teams] use [product] to [outcome]"

**Subheadline:**
- Expand on the headline with specifics.
- Address the "how" briefly.
- Keep to 1-2 sentences.

**Hero CTA:**
- Use action verbs that describe the outcome, not the process.
- Good: "Start free trial," "See it in action," "Get your report"
- Bad: "Submit," "Learn more," "Click here"

### Social Proof Bar
- Place immediately after the hero or integrated within it.
- Logos of recognizable customers.
- Key stat: "Trusted by X teams" or "Y% average improvement."
- Keep it tight — 4-6 logos, one stat.

### Problem Section
- Articulate the pain the reader feels right now.
- Use their language. Be specific about the frustration.
- This section should make the reader think: "Yes, that's exactly my problem."
- Do NOT mention your product here. Stay in the reader's world.

### Solution Section
- Transition from problem to your product as the answer.
- Show, don't just tell — use screenshots, diagrams, or short demos.
- Frame around 2-4 key capabilities, each tied to an outcome.

### Proof Section
- Testimonials with specific results, not generic praise.
- Case study snippets with before/after metrics.
- Trust badges, certifications, or security credentials if relevant.
- Bad testimonial: "Great product, love it!"
- Good testimonial: "We cut onboarding time from 3 weeks to 4 days. Our team actually uses the tool now." — Name, Title, Company

### Objection Handling
- FAQ section or dedicated content blocks that address top 3-5 objections.
- Common objections: price, complexity, switching cost, security, "we already have something."
- Answer objections directly. Don't dance around them.

### Final CTA Section
- Restate the core value proposition in one line.
- Repeat the primary CTA.
- Add a secondary low-commitment option if appropriate (e.g., "Or talk to sales").
- Create gentle urgency if authentic (limited availability, pricing changes, etc.).

## Step 5: CTA Copy Guidelines

### Primary CTA Rules
- Start with an action verb.
- Describe the outcome or next step, not the mechanism.
- Keep to 2-5 words.
- Match the commitment level to where the reader is in the funnel.

### CTA Hierarchy
- **High intent:** "Start free trial," "Get started free," "Buy now"
- **Medium intent:** "See demo," "Watch how it works," "See pricing"
- **Low intent:** "Learn more," "Read the guide," "See examples"

### Supporting Text (Microcopy)
- Add reassurance below the CTA button.
- "No credit card required" / "Free 14-day trial" / "Set up in 2 minutes"
- Reduce friction and perceived risk.

## Step 6: Page-Specific Guidance

### Homepage
- Speak to your broadest viable audience.
- Lead with the category and key differentiator.
- Provide clear navigation paths to deeper pages (use cases, pricing, resources).
- Show breadth of value — don't go deep on any single feature.

### Landing Page (Campaign-Specific)
- Match the headline to the ad or email that sent them here.
- Remove navigation — this is a single-purpose page.
- One CTA, repeated 2-3 times.
- Keep it focused: one audience, one offer, one action.

### Pricing Page
- Lead with the value, not the price.
- Use plan names that signal who each tier is for (Starter, Growth, Enterprise).
- Highlight the most popular plan visually.
- Anchor with the highest-priced plan if selling to enterprise.
- Include a comparison table for feature differences.
- Add an FAQ addressing pricing objections.

### Feature Page
- Open with the problem this feature solves.
- Show the feature in context (screenshots, GIFs, or video).
- Connect to outcomes: "This means you can..."
- Include a mini case study or testimonial specific to this feature.
- Link to related features or use cases.

### About Page
- Tell the origin story through the lens of the customer's problem.
- "We built [product] because we saw [problem] and believed [vision]."
- Include team photos and real bios — not corporate headshots with generic titles.
- State your values only if they're specific and actionable.

## Step 7: Voice and Tone Calibration

### Voice (Consistent)
Voice is your brand personality. It stays the same across all content. Define it with 3-4 adjectives:
- Example: "Direct, confident, warm, practical"
- Anti-example: "Professional" (too vague to be useful)

### Tone (Variable)
Tone shifts based on context:
- **Error messages:** Empathetic, helpful, brief.
- **Onboarding:** Encouraging, clear, patient.
- **Sales pages:** Confident, specific, outcome-focused.
- **Support docs:** Neutral, precise, step-by-step.

### Calibration Questions
When unsure about tone, ask:
- What emotion is the reader feeling right now?
- What emotion should they feel after reading this?
- What would a trusted advisor say in this moment?

## Step 8: Output Format

### Deliver Copy With:
1. **The copy itself** — ready to use, properly formatted with clear section labels.
2. **Annotations** — brief notes explaining why specific choices were made (e.g., "Leading with the pain point here because traffic is problem-aware from the ad").
3. **Alternatives** — provide 2-3 headline options and CTA variations so the user can A/B test.
4. **Assumptions** — list any assumptions made due to missing context, so the user can correct them.

### Example Output Structure
```
## Hero Section

**Headline (Option A):** [Primary recommendation]
**Headline (Option B):** [Alternative angle]
**Headline (Option C):** [Alternative angle]

> Annotation: Option A leads with the outcome because the audience
> is solution-aware. Option B tests a problem-agitation angle.

**Subheadline:** [Supporting text]

**CTA:** [Button text]
**CTA microcopy:** [Text below button]

---

## Problem Section
[Copy]

> Annotation: Language pulled from [source/assumption about customer voice]

## Solution Section
[Copy]

[Continue for each section...]
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Writing for the CEO, not the customer.** The CEO's favorite phrase is not the customer's language.
2. **Burying the value proposition.** If the reader has to scroll to understand what you do, you've lost them.
3. **Using superlatives without proof.** "Best," "leading," "world-class" — these mean nothing without evidence.
4. **Feature dumping.** Listing every feature signals you don't know which ones matter.
5. **Ignoring the awareness level.** A reader who searched "best CRM for startups" needs different copy than one who clicked a retargeting ad.
6. **Writing long paragraphs.** Dense text signals "this will be hard to read." Break it up.
7. **Generic social proof.** "Trusted by thousands" is weaker than "Trusted by 2,400 SaaS teams."
8. **Matching CTA to the wrong stage.** Don't ask someone to "Buy now" if they just learned you exist.
